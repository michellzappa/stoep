# Personas

Day-in-the-life scenarios. Each persona walks through a typical week on Stoep to ground the abstract [[Concepts|concepts]] in concrete interactions. These are the artefacts to use in co-design sessions and in pitches — residents and policy-makers respond to stories faster than to primitives.

All personas are composite, not real people. Names and details are plausible but invented.

## Aisha — 34, Indische Buurt

Moroccan-Dutch, born in Amsterdam, parent of two (6 and 9), works part-time in a GGD health education role. Lives in a huurwoning on Javastraat. Bilingual NL/AR, understands EN.

**Motivations:** wants to know what's happening in her buurt for her kids, cares about playgrounds and schools, occasionally involved in the buurtvereniging.

**Monday evening, after bedtime**
Opens Stoep on her phone. Scrolls the buurt chronological feed — two posts since Sunday. Her neighbor three doors down is looking for a bike seat for a toddler; Aisha has one gathering dust. Replies, arranges pickup Wednesday. Sees a new buurtbudget proposal to extend the speeltuin hours, reads the proposal, comments with a parent's perspective. The proposal is posted in Dutch; she reads it fine but types her comment in a mix — Stoep auto-detects and offers to translate for others.

**Wednesday**
On her walk to the school she notices a broken swing at the speeltuin. Opens Stoep, long-presses the map at the speeltuin location, creates a melding. The melding is formatted automatically with location and a photo, submitted through Signalen. She sees it appear in the buurt feed immediately, tagged "melding submitted." Three other parents confirm within an hour.

**Friday**
At the buurthuis picking up her kids from swim, sees the printed weekly buurt-digest on the noticeboard. The digest lists: 4 meldingen resolved (including her swing — the gemeente marked it "in behandeling"), 1 upcoming buurtborrel, 1 Stadsarchief photo — the Javastraat in 1962, a butcher's shop where the kapper now stands. She photographs the digest to show her husband. Her mother, who doesn't use a smartphone much, looks at the same digest when she visits the buurthuis for a taalcafé.

**What Stoep gave her:** ambient awareness, an easy way to report and see resolution, a low-key way to exchange stuff with neighbors, a moment of buurt-history delight.

**What it did not demand:** a daily check, a post, a performance of civic engagement.

## Henk — 78, Van der Pekbuurt

Widower. Lived in Noord for 51 years. Limited smartphone use — uses WhatsApp for grandchildren. Does not have a Stoep account and may never have one directly. *Is a Stoep user anyway.*

**His interface is mostly offline.**

**Saturday afternoon**
Henk walks to the buurthuis for his weekly coffee group. The Stoep printed digest is on the noticeboard, updated every Saturday morning. He reads: two new posts from neighbors looking for help with small jobs, an upcoming pop-up market next Saturday, the meldingen log for the week, one historical photo. The photo is from his street in 1970 — he remembers the shop in the photo.

**Tuesday morning**
He receives an SMS from Stoep's fallback service: *"Deze donderdag is het restafval-dag (rolcontainer aan de straat vóór 7u). — Stoep buurtdigest."* He appreciates the reminder; he used to miss bin days.

**Thursday evening**
His buurtconnector — a younger neighbor named Farida who runs the buurtborrel — stops by on her round. She mentions the speeltuin-hours proposal is open for consultation; she'd value his view. He gives her his opinion verbally; she logs it in her account as "input from a senior resident (with consent)." His voice is heard without requiring him to operate software.

**What Stoep gave him:** visibility into his buurt, reliable reminders for the logistics that drift when you live alone, a route for his voice into civic decisions. **All of this via the printed digest, SMS, and a human buurtconnector. Stoep's design assumes Henk exists.**

## Mehmet — 52, Javastraat

Runs *Bakkerij Anatolia* on Javastraat, Indische Buurt. Lives elsewhere (Bos en Lommer) but has worked in the buurt for 22 years. Non-resident but deeply of the buurt.

**Holds a `bedrijfspaspoort`** — limited-participation account tied to his KVK-registered business address in the buurt.

**Monday morning**
Early — he's been at the bakery since 5. Posts a short message via his `bedrijfspaspoort`: *"Deze week vers gebakken simit op dinsdag en donderdag. Ook Ramadan-broodjes beschikbaar."* The post appears in the feed tagged "Werkt in de buurt — Bakkerij Anatolia." A small tag makes clear it is a local business post, not a resident post.

**Wednesday**
A resident posts a question about late-night noise near the supermarket two doors down. Mehmet does not reply — that's a resident conversation, and his role stops at buurt-relevant business info. The platform's design reinforces the boundary gently; the reply box on resident-only threads shows "Alleen voor buurtbewoners."

**Friday**
The Stoep printed digest arrives at his bakery (as it does in every local business that opts in). He tapes it to the wall next to the cash register. Customers read it while waiting for bread.

**What Stoep gave him:** a way to announce buurt-relevant things directly to neighbors, without the hostility of "commercial post" stigma because the rules are clear and shared. Not a marketing channel; a buurt-presence channel.

**What it did not give him:** generic advertising reach. And he's fine with that; buurt trust is worth more than impressions.

## Lena — 27, Zuid, shortstay

Polish expat, arrived 4 months ago for a Dutch master's programme. Sublet room from a professor who travels often. Not BAG-registered at this address; her temporary registration is with the gemeente at a university address.

**Read-only visitor at first.**

**Day 3 of discovering Stoep**
A friend mentions a buurt app. Lena goes to stoep.amsterdam, sees her buurt's public posts and the civic layer — events, meldingen, the weekly digest. She can read everything. Can't post. She can sign up for a read-only visitor account with just email; the onboarding explicitly says: *"Welcome. Because you don't have a BAG registration yet, you can read and attend events. When you're ready to participate fully, here are three ways to verify."*

**Week 4**
She attends the buurtborrel listed on Stoep. At the borrel, she meets a buurtconnector who explains she can get a vouched tier if two verified neighbors vouch — the professor-landlord and one other. Professor willing, other neighbor willing after a chat.

**Week 6**
She has a vouched tier. She posts her first thing — a question about bike repair shops. Three replies within 30 minutes. She feels part of something for the first time since arriving.

**What Stoep gave her:** a welcoming, non-punishing path in. Not "you're not verified, you're second class" but "here's how to become full member; in the meantime, read, attend, meet people."

**What it did not do:** require a BAG registration she doesn't yet have, or exclude her because of bureaucratic shape.

## Farida — 41, Indische Buurt, buurtconnector

Dutch, parents of Surinamese origin. Works for a housing corporation during the day. Has been running a WhatsApp buurtgroep since 2018 with 280 neighbors. Became one of the 3 paid buurtconnectors for the Stoep pilot.

**Her work is partly on the platform, partly in the buurt.**

**Daily**
Checks Stoep twice daily (morning, evening). Reads the moderation queue — 2–3 flagged items most days. Mostly quick decisions: hiding a post, replying to de-escalate, nudging someone toward a norm. Rare: bringing a thread to the buurt council.

**Weekly**
Meets with the other two buurtconnectors on Tuesday evenings for 45 minutes at the buurthuis. Debriefs incidents, shares what's working, talks about residents needing outreach. Monthly buurtcouncil meeting, broader agenda.

**During the month**
Onboards 3–5 new residents via in-person conversations — people who heard about Stoep from the printed digest, from a neighbor, from the buurtvereniging. She knows her buurt; she knows who's likely to be lonely, who needs help, who is the quiet anchor of a straat. She routes Stoep to them, and she knows when not to.

**Compensation:** €400/month during the pilot for an estimated 10 hours/week, paid from the project budget. She knows this is below market for her professional skills but sees it as civic work.

**What Stoep gave her:** a platform for the work she was already doing, and legitimate compensation for it. A visible civic role.

**What it cost her:** enough time that burnout is a real risk — hence the rotation, the compensation, the council structure.

## What the personas teach

A platform designed for *only* Aisha is Nextdoor with Dutch skin. Designing also for Henk (offline-first) breaks the "digital-only" failure pattern. Designing also for Mehmet (clear-role non-resident) prevents both commercial takeover and the other failure of excluding buurt-stakeholders. Designing also for Lena (welcoming shortstay) makes Stoep open without diluting trust. Designing also for Farida (the civic worker) makes the platform survivable.

Each persona stress-tests a different [[Concepts|concept]]:

| Persona | Stress-tests |
|---|---|
| Aisha | Daily-use primitives, language, buurtbudget integration |
| Henk | [[Concepts|Offline bridges]], SMS fallback, buurtconnector routing |
| Mehmet | `bedrijfspaspoort`, [[Governance|commercial boundaries]] |
| Lena | [[Identity-edge-cases|Tiered verification]], welcoming shortstay |
| Farida | [[Governance|Moderation operations]], buurtconnector sustainability |

Co-design sessions should test every persona against every significant design decision. If a decision works for Aisha but fails Henk, it's not a decision yet.
